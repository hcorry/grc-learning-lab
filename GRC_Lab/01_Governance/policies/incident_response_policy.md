## 3. Roles & Responsibilities

<!-- YOUR WORK: Incident response involves a team. Define each role clearly.

Key roles for a startup-sized incident response team:
- **CISO** — owns the incident response program, leads response for major incidents
- **Incident Response Lead** — coordinates the response, manages the timeline (could be the CISO at a small company)
- **IT/Engineering** — technical investigation, containment, and remediation
- **Department Heads** — communicate with their teams, provide subject matter expertise
- **Legal** — advise on notification requirements, regulatory obligations
- **HR** — involved if the incident involves an insider or affects employees
- **Communications/PR** — external communications if the incident becomes public
- **All Employees** — report suspected incidents immediately

For a 100-person startup, some of these roles may be combined (e.g., the CISO is also the IR Lead). That's fine — document it.
-->

## 4. Policy Statements

### 4.1 Preparation
(What SecureTech does BEFORE an incident — training, tools, runbooks, contact lists, etc.)

### 4.2 Identification / Detection
(How incidents are discovered — monitoring, employee reports, external notifications. Include reporting requirements: how do employees report? How quickly must they report?)

(Immediate actions to stop the incident from spreading — isolate affected systems, disable compromised accounts, preserve evidence. Note: short-term containment first, then long-term.)

### 4.4 Eradication
(Remove the root cause — eliminate malware, close vulnerabilities, patch systems.)

### 4.5 Recovery
(Restore systems to normal operation — verify systems are clean, restore from backups if needed, monitor for recurrence.)

### 4.6 Lessons Learned / Post-Incident Review
(After the incident is resolved — conduct a post-mortem, document findings, update procedures. This is where you get better for next time.)

### 4.7 Severity Classification
(Define severity levels so the response is proportionate:)
- **Critical** — active data breach, systems down, customer data exposed
- **High** — malware detected, unauthorized access confirmed, significant risk
- **Medium** — phishing attempt partially successful, policy violation detected
- **Low** — suspicious activity, failed attack, minor policy violation

### 4.8 Communication and Notification
(Who gets notified at each severity level? Internal: executive team, legal, board. External: customers, regulators, law enforcement. What are the timelines?)
