## 1. Purpose

This policy establishes the information security framework for SecureTech Inc. It defines the organization's commitment to protecting the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of all information assets. This policy serves as the top-level "constitution" from which all other security policies derive their authority.

> 💡 **Why this section matters:** The Purpose tells the reader *why* this policy exists. It should connect security to business needs. Auditors check that every policy has a clear purpose statement.

---

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to:
- All SecureTech Inc. employees (full-time and part-time)
- Contractors and temporary workers with access to company systems
- Third-party vendors who process, store, or transmit SecureTech data
- All information assets, including data, systems, networks, and physical facilities

This policy applies regardless of where work is performed, including remote work environments.

> 💡 **Why this section matters:** Scope prevents the "I didn't know it applied to me" defense. Be explicit about who's covered. For a startup with remote workers and contractors, make sure remote work and third parties are included.

---

## 3. Roles & Responsibilities

| Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|
| **CEO / Executive Team** | Approve the information security program; allocate budget and resources; set the tone from the top that security is a business priority |
| **CISO** | Own this policy and the overall security program; conduct annual policy reviews; report security posture to the executive team |
| **Department Heads** | Ensure their teams comply with security policies; identify department-specific risks; participate in security reviews |
| **IT / Engineering** | Implement technical security controls; manage access provisioning; maintain security tools and monitoring |
| **All Employees** | Follow security policies and procedures; complete security awareness training; report suspected incidents immediately |
| **Human Resources** | Ensure security policies are part of onboarding and offboarding; support enforcement actions |

> 💡 **Why this section matters:** Roles & Responsibilities establishes accountability. Without it, everyone assumes security is "someone else's job." Auditors specifically look for role definitions.

## 4. Policy Statements

### 4.1 Information Security Program

SecureTech Inc. must maintain a formal information security program that includes:
- A risk management process for identifying, assessing, and treating information security risks
- Security policies covering all major domains (access control, incident response, data protection, acceptable use)
- Regular security awareness training for all employees
- Incident response capabilities for detecting and responding to security events
- Continuous monitoring of security controls and compliance obligations

### 4.2 Asset Management

All information assets must be:
- Inventoried and assigned an owner responsible for their protection
- Classified according to the Data Classification Policy
- Protected with controls appropriate to their classification level

### 4.3 Access Control

Access to information systems and data must be:
- Granted based on the principle of least privilege (only the minimum access needed to perform job duties)
- Approved by the resource owner or their delegate
- Reviewed quarterly and revoked when no longer needed
- Authenticated using multi-factor authentication (MFA) for all remote access and privileged accounts

### 4.4 Security Awareness

All employees and contractors must:
- Complete security awareness training within 30 days of hire
- Complete annual refresher training
- Acknowledge this policy and related policies annually

### 4.5 Third-Party Security

Third-party vendors with access to SecureTech data must:
- Undergo a security assessment before being granted access
- Maintain security controls equivalent to SecureTech requirements
- Report security incidents affecting SecureTech data within 24 hours

### 4.6 Continuous Improvement

The information security program must:
- Be reviewed at least annually for effectiveness
- Incorporate lessons learned from incidents and near-misses
- Adapt to changes in the threat landscape, business operations, and regulatory requirements

> 💡 **Why this section matters:** Policy Statements are the core of the policy — the actual rules. Notice the language: "must" for requirements. Each statement is specific enough to verify but general enough that it won't need updating every month.

---

## 5. Enforcement

Violations of this policy may result in disciplinary action, up to and including:
- Verbal or written warning
- Mandatory additional security training
- Suspension of system access
- Termination of employment or contract
- Legal action where applicable

All suspected violations must be reported to the CISO. Enforcement actions will be proportionate to the severity of the violation and conducted in coordination with Human Resources.

> 💡 **Why this section matters:** A policy without enforcement is just a suggestion. This section gives the policy teeth. It also protects the organization legally — employees can't claim they didn't know there were consequences.

## 6. Review Schedule

| Activity | Frequency | Responsible Party |
|---|---|---|
| Full policy review | Annually | CISO |
| Update for regulatory changes | As needed | CISO + Legal |
| Update after major incidents | As needed | CISO |

This policy was approved by the SecureTech Inc. executive team.

> 💡 **Why this section matters:** Frameworks like ISO 27001 and SOC 2 require documented review schedules. "Annually" is the standard cadence. Including "as needed" triggers (incidents, regulatory changes) shows you think about real-world scenarios.
